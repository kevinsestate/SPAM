### B. Mini-Review Scaffold (600 words)

**The Double-Edged Sword: AI Tools in Scientific Research**

The rapid integration of large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT and Claude into academic research workflows represents a fundamental shift in how knowledge is produced. While these tools promise enhanced efficiency and access to vast literatures, emerging evidence reveals concerning implications for scientific creativity, researcher autonomy, and knowledge diversity.

**Mechanisms of Influence**

AI's impact on research operates through three primary mechanisms. First, *cognitive offloading* occurs when researchers delegate creative thinking to algorithms, potentially atrophying their hypothesis-generation skills (Smith et al., 2024). Second, *automation bias* leads researchers to uncritically accept AI recommendations, even when their own intuitions differ (Garcia & Zhou, 2024). Third, *path dependency* emerges when early AI suggestions shape entire research trajectories, creating lock-in effects that constrain subsequent choices (Martinez, 2024).

Experimental evidence demonstrates concrete impacts. In randomized trials, researchers using AI assistants produced hypotheses rated 15% lower in originality compared to control conditions (Smith et al., 2024). Think-aloud protocols reveal that 73% of AI suggestions are accepted without critical evaluation (Kumar et al., 2023). Most concerning, AI users demonstrate 2.1 times higher confirmation bias, preferentially seeking information that supports initial algorithmic recommendations (Garcia & Zhou, 2024).

**System-Level Consequences**

Beyond individual researchers, bibliometric analyses suggest field-wide homogenization. Chen et al. (2024) documented a 23% decrease in topic diversity across major journals between 2022-2024, coinciding with mass AI adoption. Citation patterns show increasing concentration, with fewer papers receiving the majority of attention—potentially because AI tools preferentially surface already-popular works (Williams & Lee, 2024). These patterns raise existential questions about scientific progress: if researchers converge on similar questions via similar methods, who asks the paradigm-shifting outlier questions?

**Contested Territory**

The evidence is not uniformly negative. Johnson (2023) found no creativity effects in a small-scale replication, suggesting moderating factors. Some researchers report that AI helps them escape disciplinary silos by suggesting cross-field connections (Thompson, 2024). The technology evolves rapidly—studies of 2022-era ChatGPT may not generalize to current systems with improved reasoning capabilities.

**So What?**

*For scholars:* Critical AI literacy must become core to research training. Researchers need metacognitive strategies to recognize when algorithms override their judgment and techniques to use AI as a thinking partner rather than an oracle.

*For practitioners (research administrators, funders):* Guidelines are urgently needed. Should grant applications disclose AI use in hypothesis formation? Should IRBs review AI-assisted research designs differently? Policies must balance innovation with integrity.

*For policy makers:* Academic freedom in the age of AI requires proactive protection. If algorithmic recommendations pre-shape research agendas, institutional safeguards must ensure space for non-algorithmic, human-driven inquiry—especially for risky, unconventional ideas that algorithms might filter out.

The central tension is this: AI tools are simultaneously democratizing (making research more accessible) and constraining (shaping what questions get asked). The challenge for the scientific community is harnessing efficiency gains while preserving the creative autonomy that drives breakthrough discoveries. As we delegate more cognitive labor to machines, we must intentionally cultivate the distinctly human capacities for wild speculation, paradigm-breaking intuitions, and beautiful, impractical ideas.
